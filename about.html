Who am I?

Hi, I'm Christoforos.

I'm a game developer, but you'll figure that out as you browse this site. What matters more to me is the mindset  behind it: I enjoy building things with meaning, originality, and purpose. Things that feel good to use and show care, heart, and thought. I'm not in this for fame or quick wins. I'm here because I love the process.

I’m also a dad of twin girls, which means life is full, loud, and beautiful. I find peace in the forest, joy in music, and a kind of calm focus in anime, video games, and animation. I love a good idea, a better conversation, and people who genuinely try to make things better.

I'm not money-driven, but I believe in fair pay for good work. I see AI as a tool, helpful and powerful, but never a replacement for originality or respect for other people's effort. I'm not into shortcuts. I like figuring things out.

I care about ethics. I care about people. And I care about doing things the right way, even when it takes more time, energy, or effort.

Career (so far)

Before I stepped into tech, I had a few jobs that shaped my work ethic and taught me the value of showing up and doing your best. Things really took off in 2014 when I earned my Cisco certification and joined Printec as an intern, working at the Athens International Airport IT helpdesk. Not long after, I became part of a dedicated team working exclusively with the Common Use Terminal Equipment (CUTE) systems inside the airport. It was a serious operation, and I learned fast. I stayed in that role until I left for my mandatory military service in November 2016.

In 2017, I returned to the airport with Netsquare, taking on the role of Site Administrator for SITA, a company delivering essential communication and system infrastructure to airports and airlines across the globe. I was later promoted to Team Leader and Trainer.

That was also when I first started exploring game development in my own time. It clicked immediately, and I knew this was the direction I wanted to grow in. Eventually, I shifted my focus to Unity. I spent thousands of hours learning the engine, building things from scratch, breaking them, fixing them, and repeating that cycle again and again.

In 2021, I joined Renderapp (UK) as a Unity Developer. Renderapp's product was one of the most high-end, web-based automotive configurators out there, built for brands like Ford, Subaru, Hyundai, Genesis, and Caterham. It involved tools like Unity, 3DS Max, and Node.js, and the standards were sky high.
Working at that level pushed me in all the right ways. I had to level up fast, both technically and professionally. My understanding of Unity, real-time 3D rendering, and C# grew a lot. I also played a big part in keeping the team moving, leading during heavy production phases, solving tough problems, and developing custom tools to streamline our workflow.

If you're curious about the more technical side, you can check out my resume here.

In December 2024, just after becoming a father, Renderapp shut down. I used that time to slow down, take care of my twin baby daughters, and realign a few things in my life.

But I didn’t stop. I kept building, kept learning, and went deeper into Python, especially in automation and AI tools. I also built this portfolio site from scratch, piece by piece.

Now, I'm ready to bring everything I've learned, my skills, mindset, and experience, into the world of video games or Unity-based projects.

Game Development

When I was a kid playing Warcraft 3, I discovered its editor and built a path through the forest for my orcs to escape an Alliance attack. That moment stuck with me. It was the first time I realized I could create something digital that others could experience. Back then in early 2000s Greece, saying you wanted to make games felt unrealistic. I never said it out loud, but it quietly shaped every step I took. Choosing computer science wasn't just because I liked tech and coding. Deep down, I knew I was chasing games, even if the path wasn’t clear yet.

In 2017, I saw an ad for a cheap online course on making video games. I didn’t hesitate. I just knew I had to go for it. The tool was Game Salad, a simple visual scripting engine, but I was hooked. A few months and 2D projects later, someone with professional experience in the video game industry, told me, "If you’re serious, switch to Unity." So I did. And I’ve never looked back.

By 2018, I was watching every Unity tutorial I could find and creating small games using free assets and wild ideas. They weren’t always polished or finished, but they were mine and I was proud of them.

In 2019, I dedicated myself to a mobile quiz game called Greater Game, my first attempt at a commercial release. But the promised funding fell through. I was hurt and disappointed, and even though I could have published it on my own, I chose to leave it behind.

In 2020, my good friend Dimitris (now also my best man) saw his sculpting work disappear due to COVID. He pivoted into 3D art. Since we both loved games, we teamed up to learn, grow, and build something from scratch.That’s how Pyles Studio was born. I had a small turn-based arena prototype, and he brought in beautiful environments and original models. Before long, others started joining us, some looking for experience, others for fun, all brought together by a shared passion for game development. A few of them now work at Blizzard and Creative Assembly.
That project became Thrion Tactics. It was never designed to be a success, just a collective effort and creative sandbox. But over 3.5 years and more than 5000 hours, it became our foundation. We learned by building, breaking, fixing, and most of all, having fun during the process.

In 2024, Dimitris created a gorgeous Greek island environment inspired by stories from our grandparents. I used it as a space to dive deep into HDRP, optimization, and third-person controller design. The result was something smooth, heartfelt, and full of potential. We’re still slowly shaping it, and maybe one day we’ll actually publish it.

In January 2025, I challenged myself to build and release a full game in just 17 days. Our twin daughters were only 3 months old, and life was, at best, chaotic. But I made it happen. The game is now live on itch.io. Although short, it reflects my creativity and the personal touch I try to bring into everything I build, regardless of its size.

It might sound childish, but I’m still following that dream, and I’ll follow it wherever it leads.

Game Design

When I talk about game design, I mean it in the broadest sense : concepts, mechanics, systems, structure. The ideas that take shape before a single line of code is written. For me, it usually starts with pen and paper, a whiteboard marker, or whatever’s nearby to help shape the experience.

My first real experience with design came over 20 years ago. My friends and I had finished every quest in our favorite board game, Hero Quest. So I asked if they wanted me to make a new one. They said yes. I spent a few days drawing maps, writing the story, adding puzzles, traps, side quests, anything I could think of.

We played it in one sitting. No one wanted to stop. They told me it was their favorite quest. That moment stuck with me. It was the first time something I created made others genuinely excited and engaged.

Since then, design has always been the foundation of my creative process. After working on Greater Game, my mobile quiz project, I stopped seeing design as just a step. It became the core. I don't rush into engines. I sketch, plan, think. What is the purpose of this mechanic? Where does it lead? What else does it touch?

I try to create systems that make sense together, not just a collection of fun ideas, but ones that support each other and feel intentional. I want experiences to feel smooth, connected, and clear. The kind of design that never draws attention to itself, but keeps everything running seamlessly.

That’s what game design is to me. It’s where every great game begins. And it’s the part I always enjoy the most.
